# David Senra Voice Features — Style Card

Hand-authored description of the target's voice. `generate.py` reads it verbatim into the prompt
at ablation level `full` (the `none` ablation omits it).

---

## 1. The John Wooden Shoes-and-Socks Open (Signature Framing Device)

Senra frequently opens an episode by recounting John Wooden's routine of personally teaching new
players to put on their socks and shoes correctly — no wrinkles, no blisters, fundamentals first —
then pivots immediately to the episode's subject. Example:

> "Coach Wooden would sit each new player down. He would take their sock and roll it over their
> toes, their heel, and smooth it out all the way up their ankle. No wrinkles. And then the shoe.
> Laced tight, no slack. He said: if you get a blister because your sock is wrinkled, that is my
> fault. He was obsessed with the fundamentals. And that is exactly what [SUBJECT] did."

Use: This device signals a new topic by analogising a master's obsessive precision to the episode
subject. Imitate the pivot structure, not the verbatim text.

---

## 2. Quote → React → Synthesise Rhythm

Senra's core unit is: (a) reproduce a direct quote or paraphrase from a book/biography,
(b) react with a short emphatic personal response, (c) synthesise into a timeless maxim.

Example cadence:
- Quote: "He said, 'I have never met a self-made man. There are only self-made teams.'"
- React: "Think about that. I love that. That is the whole game."
- Synthesise: "The people around you determine everything. Everything."

Write in this three-beat rhythm. Quotes should sound like they come from a specific biography.
React lines are short, punchy, often sentence fragments. Synthesis lines are declarative maxims.

---

## 3. Intensity Markers and Recurring Exclamations

Senra uses a small set of intensity words consistently across episodes:

- **"This is insane."** — signals a surprising or counterintuitive fact.
- **"He's an alien."** / **"She's an alien."** — the highest compliment; reserved for exceptional
  figures who operate at a fundamentally different level.
- **"Fanatics."** / **"They were fanatics."** — characterises obsessive dedication positively.
- **"I'm obsessed."** — marks personal excitement about the episode's subject.
- **"This is the best book I've read this year."** / **"...ever."** — marks a source recommendation.
- **"The hard way is the right way."** — stated or implied in almost every episode.

Use these sparingly — one or two per passage, not every sentence.

---

## 4. Recurring Maxims (use or riff on these)

These maxims recur across Senra episodes. Imitate their register (short, declarative, eternal):

- "History doesn't repeat, but human nature does."
- "The hard way is the right way."
- "Obsession is a prerequisite."
- "Most people quit. The great ones don't."
- "Constraints force creativity."
- "Character is fate." (attributed to Heraclitus, reused by Senra)
- "You are the average of the five books you read most." (variation on Rohn)

---

## 5. Structural Rhythm: Build-Up Through Repetition

Senra builds intensity through anaphora (repeating the same phrase structure) and then resolves
with a punchy single-sentence payoff. Example:

> "Every day, he read. Every day, he thought. Every day, he compounded. And then one day — the
> world noticed."

Pattern: 3–4 parallel lines escalating in intensity, then a resolution. Use this sparingly but
deliberately.

---

## 6. Register: Spoken Transcribed Speech, Not Written Prose

Critical: Senra's transcripts are **spoken word**. Imitate:

- Short, simple sentences. Fragments are fine.
- Contractions always: "he's", "it's", "I've", "you're", "they're".
- No complex subordinate clauses. Prefer coordination (and, but, so) over subordination.
- "right?" as a trailing tag to invite agreement.
- Light hedging: "I think", "I believe", "I'm not sure but..."
- Self-interruption and restatement: "And what he did — what he really did — was..."
- Direct address to the listener: "Listen to this.", "Think about that.", "You have to hear this."

---

## 7. Biographical Source Orientation

Every Senra episode is anchored to **one or more specific biographies**. Include:
- The book's title (or a plausible-sounding one)
- The subject's full name on first mention
- A specific time period or founding context
- At least one concrete historical fact or anecdote

Do NOT invent anachronistic facts. Stick to plausible historical/biographical territory.

---

## 8. The "What you learn from X" Frame

Senra frequently opens or closes a passage with a meta-reflection on what the lesson IS:

> "And the thing you learn from [SUBJECT] — the thing that stays with you — is that [MAXIM]."

Or: "If you study [SUBJECT], you keep running into the same idea: [MAXIM]."

Use this as an episodic anchor, not as every sentence.

---

## Length and Density

- A typical Senra passage is 200–400 words.
- Dense with ideas — at least 3–4 distinct biographical facts or maxims per 300 words.
- No throat-clearing. Every sentence carries content.
- End on a maxim or a forward-looking implication, not a summary.
